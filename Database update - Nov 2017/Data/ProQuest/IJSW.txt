Author: Otto, Hans-Uwe1; Schaarschuch, A1 Fakultat Padagogik, Centre Social Service Studies, U Bielefeld, Germany

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61658611?accountid=14667

Abstract: Examines developments in German social work theory, 1970s-1990s, starting with an analysis of socioeconomic relations that have given rise to a division of society as well as a consumerist approach of service orientation for the social services -- ie, the privileging of the consumer -- that presents a challenge both for social work theory & professional practice. Against this background, major themes in the development of social work theory are identified, including a trend toward greater recognition of individuals & their lifeworlds. Although presented in the ideological context of neoliberalism, the core of the consumerist approach of service orientation provides a basis for a paradigm shift in social work theory that will be an adequate response to the reflexive modernization of society if taken out of the consumerist context & reformulated for the public services. Outlined is a theory of "social work as service" as a professional model of action in the public sector of social service provision. 39 References. Adapted from the source document.

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61643409?accountid=14667

Abstract: Managed care has created a new service environment, eliminating distinctions that have sustained social work ideology & the profession throughout its history. After reviewing important features of managed care, considered here are changes that it has brought to practice ideology, the practitioner-client relationship, & the practice environment, including social work employment. The new opportunities, & challenges that have emerged with this approach are discussed. While social workers should not embrace managed care, they should not blindly oppose it, but rather, aggressively pursue it, fighting its major drawbacks, while adapting to a competitive marketplace. Given the current managed care market, the social work profession is now in the right place, at the right time, with the right skills, & available at the right price. It must take on a leadership role to maintain its competitive position, protect its clients, & ensure quality practice. 29 References. Adapted from the source document.

Incorporating Gender Inequality into Income Distribution Research

Author: Fritzell, Johan11 Instit Social Research, Stockholm U, Sweden

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61635679?accountid=14667

Abstract: Income distribution research assumes equal sharing of resources in the household. This assumption has been criticized for leading to an underestimation of the extent of gender inequality in modern societies. Here, two alternative methods of deriving an income distribution -- (1) a partial sharing distribution modeled from survey data & (2) a distribution of individual income, ie, no sharing of resources -- are applied to income distribution data from the 1991 Swedish Level of Living Survey, & the respective distributions are compared with the baseline distribution, assuming equal pooling of resources. As expected, results indicate that differences between men & women increase, but the extent of this increase is somewhat less than expected. Nevertheless, the analysis highlights how gender inequality can be brought into the field of income distribution. 4 Tables, 4 Figures, 30 References. Adapted from the source document.

Breaking the Matthew Effect -- On Women Leaving Prostitution

Author: Mansson, Sven-Axel1; Hedin, U-C1 Dept Social Work, Goteborg U, Sweden

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61608400?accountid=14667

Abstract: Life-story interviews with 23 Swedish women who exited prostitution, 1981-1995, revealed that most of the subjects (Ss) had been active in the sex trade for 5+ years. The majority had experiences exclusively of street prostitution, the most common form in Sweden. Analysis shows a number of different exit courses, ranging from breakaways by Ss who were relatively loosely integrated in the milieu to breakaways made by Ss who had reached the limit of the existentially bearable. Four main challenges faced by the Ss after leaving the trade are outlined: (1) working through & understanding the experiences of a life in prostitution, (2) dealing with shame, (3) living in a marginal situation, & (4) dealing with intimate & close relationships. Presented is an integrated exit model that combines structural, situational, interpersonal, & individual factors in explaining how & why women leave prostitution. The purpose of the model is to enhance understanding of why & how certain people are able not only to survive a difficult life situation, but also to grow & develop further during the process. 1 Figure, 23 References. Author: Vinnerljung, Bo11 Centre Evaluation Social Services, National Board Health & Welfare, Stockholm, Sweden

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61649608?accountid=14667

Abstract: A commentary on "Are Girls More than Boys Subjected to Disrupted Conditions of Upbringing?" (Bergman, Lars R., & Wangby, Margit, 1998) refers to three previous studies by Vinnerljung (1995 & 1996) indicating that, compared to girls, boys are overrepresented in foster care in Sweden, making up 53% of the foster child population. Abstract: During the 1990s, the notion of "social exclusion" has given a new impetus to the debate about poverty and disadvantage. Abstract: Child welfare services have multiple goals, including child protection, family continuity, & achievement of legal permanency so children can end their involvement with such services & have a lifetime family. These goals are not all achievable to the same extent in all cases. US child welfare policy has, in the last few years, become more definitive about the priority of child protection above family preservation. Now, situations that involve safety risks that are too great do not require any efforts at reunifying children to their biological homes. Abstract: In The Social Construction of Drug Control: The Case of Sweden and the Netherlands, Mats Hilte opens this debate by demonstrating that, although these countries are seen as having opposing drug policies, they share commonalties when viewed in terms of basic objectives. In What's Missing in Drug Policy? Ted Goldberg discusses the shift in drug policy direction at the 1988 UN International Conference on Drug Abuse & Trafficking in Vienna, Austria) from a focus on reducing supply (production/distribution), to reducing demand (consumption). Differences in thinking between policies emphasizing prohibition, harm reduction, & demand reduction are described, maintaining that neither prohibition nor harm reduction addresses the roots of the problem. The relationship between drug consumption & a nation's social & economic institutions is examined, & it is suggested that more effort must be expended to find effective ways to incorporate demand reduction into drug policy thinking. 18 References.
