Masculinity Ideology, Gender Differences and Young Women's Risk for HIV Infection

Author: Bowd, Alan D1; Loos, Cynthia H1 Faculty Education Lakehead U

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61612600?accountid=14667

Abstract: Examines the role of masculine ideology in placing young women at risk for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), based on questionnaires completed by 104 female & 77 male first-year university students in Canada. Male questionnaires contained an additional 10 "masculinity ideology" items. Both sexes indicated an average age of 16.8 years at first sexual intercourse, with 33% of females & 10% of males reporting they never had sex. Females were significantly more likely to use safer sex practices, inquire about previous partners, & have fewer partners than males. While both sexes expressed similar beliefs about openness & the meaning of "no," it is suggested that interventions targeted at females should emphasize the inconsistency between male beliefs & behavior. Males scoring high on masculinity ideology were more likely to not use condoms, be dominant & uncommunicative about sex, & believe it is natural for men, but not women, to have multiple partners. It is suggested that male education should begin early & intervention strategies should confront ingrained attitudes. 3 Tables, 20 References. J. Lindroth

Are Students Prepared to Work with Persons with Developmental Disabilities?

Author: Burge, Philip1; Druick, Dwight; Caron, Marie-Claude; Ouellette-Kuntz, Helene1 Developmental Consulting Program, Queen's U, 80 Queen St, Ste 301, Kingston, Ontario, K7K 6W7

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61612653?accountid=14667

Abstract: An assessment of Canadian social work education programs focused on the degree of emphasis placed on work with developmentally disabled persons in fieldwork, maintaining that direct contact strengthens prosocial attitudes. Analysis of survey questionnaires completed by fieldwork coordinators of 17 bachelor & 15 master's programs at 22 of Canada's 31 accredited university schools of social work for the 1996/97 academic year showed that 2.4% & 5.2% of students in master's & bachelor programs, respectively, participated in fieldwork focusing on the developmentally disabled. An additional 11% of master's & 18% of bachelor students experienced limited exposure. There were significant provincial differences with the highest degree of focus in Ontario & the lowest in Quebec. Possible reasons for the differences are discussed. Lack of student interest was indicated as a limiting factor by 77% of the respondents, with lack of qualified placement sites & acceptable field instructors the next most frequently cited factors. Suggestions are made for ways to change the situation. J. Lindroth

Clients Set Boundaries Too!

Author: Cossom, Adrienne

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61631306?accountid=14667

Abstract: Explores client boundary problems in therapy, based on in-depth interviews with 15 former clients of a counseling program at a Toronto, Ontario, women's health center. The participants were well-educated, Canadian-born women of mostly Anglo-European origin, ages 21-58. Findings reveal that counseling produced considerable anxiety & fear. Clients struggled with boundary dilemmas in their interactions with the counselor, the extent of disclosure they were comfortable with, & the therapy process itself. Although the clients appreciated the feminist counseling emphasis on egalitarian client-therapist interactions, they expected appropriate limits & had differing feelings about counselor self-disclosure. Problems arising from client decisions about what to say & what to withhold are examined, along with class/gender/age/race differences & actions by therapists that made clients resist the therapy process. Distinctions between client & therapist boundary issues, & the implications of the study for practice are discussed. 15 References. J. Lindroth

Abstract: Describes an integrated model of occupational assistance designed to create both worker & workplace wellness by drawing on existing practice models coupled with renewed emphasis on self-help. The shift from the traditional model of occupational social work as a mechanism of social control to one of active social change is examined, along with the problems most frequently encountered & the evolution of self-help groups. The model presented incorporates worker participation in an ecological framework that focuses on the target (individual/organizational wellness) & the method of intervention (professional intervention & mutual aid/self-help). The core concepts of participation & self-determination are examined, along with the wide range of implementation options that allow mutual aid/self-help dimensions to become the foundation of new programs or added to existing programs. The model's flexibility in varied workplace & workforce environments, & the importance of social work involvement in occupational assistance are discussed. 2 Figures, 46 References. J. Lindroth

Author: Frechette, Lucie11 Groupe etude & recherche intervention sociale U Quebec, Hull

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61663264?accountid=14667

Abstract: Explores the issue of children & death in war/armed conflict & life-threatening work, along with how social workers from the North & South can jointly address these problems. The psychopathological consequences of war for children & intervention approaches designed to limit long-term effects are discussed, & the dangers of child labor in agriculture, the informal urban economy, & domestic service are described. It is maintained that the expertise of social workers in the South in working with war orphans, refugees, & torture victims is useful to Northern practitioners working with immigrants. Interventions used in Asia & Latin America to assist street children can also help Northern social workers in urban poverty areas. Further, social change advocates in the South rely on those from the North to join in the fight for human rights & to raise awareness of children's issues. It is argued that international solidarity is essential to address the social, economic, & political conditions that threaten children's lives. 25 References. J. Lindroth

Dichotomy between Disclosure of Sexual Abuse and the Child Welfare System's Response

Author: Oxman-Martinez, Jacqueline1; Rowe, William S; Straka, Silvia M1 Centre Applied Family Studies McGill U, Montreal

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61620548?accountid=14667

Abstract: Examines disclosure delays in 45 intrafamilial child sexual abuse cases retained by two centers in the Quebec territories of Monteregie & Saguenay Lac St. Jean during 1995, drawing on data obtained from computerized case files & questionnaires completed by 20 practitioners. All but one of the victims were female, & the majority were abused before reaching puberty; however, abuse occurred approximately 3 years before the first retained report. Most victims accepted their abuse, but only 42% of the abusers accepted responsibility, slightly more than 25% denied it, & the rest minimized it. Response by the child welfare system is discussed, & it is suggested that the time span between first abuse & first retained report may be related to difficulties young children have with disclosure. Other complexities involved in disclosure are discussed. It is argued that the loss of professionals specializing in sexual abuse, fiscal pressures, & inadequate screening procedures have led to this failure. Suggestions are made for ways to improve response. 23 References. J. Lindroth

Social Work: Our Roots, Our Future

Author: Drover, Glenn11 Maritime School Social Work Dalhousie U, Halifax

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61638470?accountid=14667

Abstract: An edited version of the keynote address presented at the June 1998 AARSW/CASW National Conference in Edmonton, Alberta, traces the history of social work in Canada. When the Canadian Assoc of Social Workers was formed in 1926, it was believed that rational intervention would right societal wrongs. The Great Depression placed social workers at the forefront of the move to create a new social order based on communitarian values & principles of solidarity. Working with Dissociative Identity Disorder from a Feminist Social Work Perspective

Author: Coholic, Diana11 Community Counselling Centre, Nipissing Ontario

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61502868?accountid=14667

Abstract: Means of treating dissociative identity disorder ([DID] previously called multiple personality disorder) are discussed from a feminist social work (FSW) perspective. DID is the product of coping strategies developed early in life as a means of dealing with severe sexual, physical, & psychological assault. FSW is based on a number of principles: recognition of the client's strength, collaborative work with the client, & promoting understanding of the impact of societal processes. Women comprise 90% of diagnosed DID cases & must be given control over their own treatment since control has been denied them throughout their lives. Under these circumstances, the counselor acts as a guide & witness rather than an expert or decision maker. An approach depicting DID women as whole beings (rather than a conglomeration of different personality parts) is advocated, arguing that this encourages internal cooperation & removes dissociative boundaries. The medical model which depicts women as sick & in need of a cure is rejected. It is concluded that counselors dedicated to FSW should also promote education & campaigns against violence toward women. 14 References. T. Sevier

Battered Woman Syndrome: Hazards and Implications

Author: Smith, Deborah11 Puketiro Centre Child/Adolescent/Family Mental Health Service, New Zealand

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61488317?accountid=14667

Abstract: Discusses the legal, clinical, & social dangers of depicting the victims of violence against women as sufferers of battered woman syndrome (BWS). The BWS concept describes both a pattern of violence against a woman by her partner (cycle of violence theory) & the psychological impact of this violence on the women (learned helplessness). Although some have suggested that BWS leads to a broader understanding of wife abuse & helps overcome many of the myths surrounding such violence, it is argued that BWS is potentially more harmful than beneficial. In both the legal & clinical contexts, the use of the term "syndrome" implies abnormality & mental illness on the part of the victim, while shifting the focus & blame away from the abuser. Furthermore, the use of BWS privileges the voice of the expert over the experience of the victim, & thereby encourages quantification & objectification of deeply disturbing personal experiences. BWS also neglects the social & political dimensions of violence against women. Despite these dangers, it is concluded that social workers can pursue & facilitate more responsible & realistic application of BWS in clinical, legal, & social settings. 21 References. T. Sevier

Abstract: Discusses the shortcomings of divorce mediation in cases involving spousal abuse against women. Abstract: Examines the strategies enlisted in the elimination of a welfare snitch line (used to report welfare fraud) in Sudbury, Ontario. Family Therapy and the Satir Model

Author: Gomori, Maria; Prud'homme, Jacqueline C

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61605945?accountid=14667

Abstract: The evolution of family therapy & the Satir model is discussed in an interview with two former students of pioneer Virginia Satir who are currently family therapists utilizing the model. It is argued that family systems are representative of larger social systems, & understanding of the family facilitates understanding of larger group, organizational, & national relationships. The Satir model rejects therapist moralizing & decisions that create dependency, & emphasizes the clients' capacity to develop their own energy, choices, & beliefs. Despite recent shifts in traditional family structures, it is suggested that the Satir model is applicable to diverse forms of family organization. Furthermore, the basic family triad of mother, father, & child is still the basic unit of learning, despite the absence of certain members at certain times. At the institutional level, family therapy is increasingly privatized, with increased emphasis on networking & the overlap of psychoanalytic, individual, & systems perspectives. T. Sevier

Author: Waldock, Thomas11 Trent U, Peterborough Ontario K9J 7B8

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61579801?accountid=14667

Abstract: Current challenges facing the Canadian foster care system are discussed in reference to the need for professionalization. The shortcomings of institutionalized care for displaced children are increasingly recognized, placing a great burden on the foster care system. It is argued that foster care has been undermined by role conflicts among foster parents who must negotiate multiple identities as parents, volunteers, clients, therapists, & staff. Furthermore, more children are entering the foster care system with special needs, & foster parents are in the best position to provide such services. Abstract: It is argued that advocacy is the key feature distinguishing social work from other helping professions, but perceptions about the extent or form it should take are inconsistent. Sexual Harassment in the Workplace: The Silent Oppression

Author: Smith, Deborah11 Paketiro Centre Child & Family Clinic, Wellington New Zealand

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61567859?accountid=14667

Abstract: An analysis of sexual harassment in the workplace, focusing on the history of harassment policy in Canada. It is argued here that three models of human behavior are typically used to explain sexual harassment: the natural, in which sexual attraction may be present; the organizational, in which workplace power conflicts are expressed in demeaning sexual overtures; & the sociocultural model, in which harassment is a reflection of the inferior social status of women. Canadian policies regarding sexual harassment began in 1890, at which time factory owners were forbade from "seducing women & girls" they employed. This restriction was amended in 1920 to apply only to "girls," & was implicitly designed to protect the "virture" of the umarried female. In 1981, the code was revised to apply to females in all industries. Canadian human rights activists are currently working toward a gender-neutral policy that protects all workers from harassment. It is suggested that social workers play an important role in advocating policy changes & clarifying inconsistencies in understanding & workplace practices. 4 Tables, 21 References. Current Issues and Future Directions in Hospital Social Work: Report of a National Survey

Author: Herbert, Margot1; Levin, Ron1 Faculty Social Work U Calgary, Alberta T2N 1N4

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61604690?accountid=14667

Abstract: In past several years, Western health care has undergone major changes in the provision of care, including the blurring of professional roles, a greater emphasis on ambulatory rather than inpatient care, & various measures designed to streamline the cost effectiveness of health service. It is argued that these constant changes present obstacles to care, which are here examined in the context of Canadian social workers. Data were gathered via a questionnaire (N = 52 respondents [Rs]) distributed to all 1992 Canadian Assoc of Social Work Administrators in Health Facilities conference registrants, seeking to ascertain current problems & opportunities in hospital social work & possibilities for change. Results show that problems centered on a lack of professional credibility & control, which 62% of Rs attributed to budget cuts & the streamlining of care. Major opportunities were seen in the ability of social workers to motivate team-centered work forces, an expansion of their treatment & research duties, & an enhancement of their role as consumer advocates. Most Rs predicted that in the future social workers will become more important, as the hospital becomes more outpatient-service-oriented, though their professional duties & identities will most likely be transformed as current departments are merged with other sectors of care service. 6 References. Child Abduction: An Aspect of Troubled Divorce

Author: Shipton, Beverley

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61637726?accountid=14667

Abstract: Questionnaire data are used to examine the experiences & emotional problems of a sample of children (N = 12, ages 9+) abducted in a divorce process, & of their custodial parent, drawn from records of agencies in Canada & the US that assist parents in retrieving children. The average age of both abducting & victimized parents was 33.9, with abductors's education level above average. Most children felt that the abduction was not motivated by love but by a desire to hurt the custodial parent. Most also felt "more mature" & independent after the experience. Parents attested that most children had emotional problems following the abduction, & all respondent parents attended at least two self-help group sessions. http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61348594?accountid=14667

Abstract: Uses mail questionnaire data from 126 social workers in full-time independent practice in Canada to examine the dramatic increase in the incidence of this type of practice & the identity, purpose, & future direction of the profession. The overwhelming judgment of respondents was that independent practice is a legitimate form of social work & that traditional agency-based social work stifles creativity & initiative & does not adequately empower clients. Author: Meloche, Monique1; Plante, Gilles C1 Dept psychiatrie U Montreal, Quebec H3C 3J7

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61344022?accountid=14667

Abstract: Uses 1988/89 observational data to show that girls ages 9-11 have serious school difficulties when living with their single welfare mothers. Two groups of girls (N = 8 each) in Canada were evaluated, one from a socially advantaged, & the other from a socially disadvantaged, background. As expected, the girls from the disadvantaged group were vulnerable, & showed poor self-expression, self-esteem, & general hopelessness. This combination of features indicates future school trouble & possible behavioral problems; early detection & preventive self-esteem building techniques are suggested.
