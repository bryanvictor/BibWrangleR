Working with Dissociative Identity Disorder from a Feminist Social Work Perspective

Author: Coholic, Diana11 Community Counselling Centre, Nipissing Ontario

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61502868?accountid=14667

Abstract: Means of treating dissociative identity disorder ([DID] previously called multiple personality disorder) are discussed from a feminist social work (FSW) perspective. DID is the product of coping strategies developed early in life as a means of dealing with severe sexual, physical, & psychological assault. FSW is based on a number of principles: recognition of the client's strength, collaborative work with the client, & promoting understanding of the impact of societal processes. Women comprise 90% of diagnosed DID cases & must be given control over their own treatment since control has been denied them throughout their lives. Under these circumstances, the counselor acts as a guide & witness rather than an expert or decision maker. An approach depicting DID women as whole beings (rather than a conglomeration of different personality parts) is advocated, arguing that this encourages internal cooperation & removes dissociative boundaries. The medical model which depicts women as sick & in need of a cure is rejected. It is concluded that counselors dedicated to FSW should also promote education & campaigns against violence toward women. 14 References. T. Sevier

Battered Woman Syndrome: Hazards and Implications

Author: Smith, Deborah11 Puketiro Centre Child/Adolescent/Family Mental Health Service, New Zealand

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61488317?accountid=14667

Abstract: Discusses the legal, clinical, & social dangers of depicting the victims of violence against women as sufferers of battered woman syndrome (BWS). The BWS concept describes both a pattern of violence against a woman by her partner (cycle of violence theory) & the psychological impact of this violence on the women (learned helplessness). Although some have suggested that BWS leads to a broader understanding of wife abuse & helps overcome many of the myths surrounding such violence, it is argued that BWS is potentially more harmful than beneficial. In both the legal & clinical contexts, the use of the term "syndrome" implies abnormality & mental illness on the part of the victim, while shifting the focus & blame away from the abuser. Furthermore, the use of BWS privileges the voice of the expert over the experience of the victim, & thereby encourages quantification & objectification of deeply disturbing personal experiences. BWS also neglects the social & political dimensions of violence against women. Despite these dangers, it is concluded that social workers can pursue & facilitate more responsible & realistic application of BWS in clinical, legal, & social settings. 21 References. T. Sevier

Abused Women in Jeopardy: The Case against Divorce Mediation

Author: Muggli, Tracy11 Saskatoon Family Support Centre, Saskatchewan

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61490805?accountid=14667

Abstract: Discusses the shortcomings of divorce mediation in cases involving spousal abuse against women. Most mediation proceedings emphasize gender neutrality, & thereby neglect the potential power imbalances inherent in cases involving violence against women. Furthermore, the principle of mediator neutrality similarly undermines issues of abuse against women by insisting that mediators ignore emotional or value judgments. Such a system ignores the structural oppression faced by women in a male-dominated society. By focusing on the future, mediation allows male abusers to escape public scrutiny, criticism, & punishment. Overcoming these problems requires a legal system that is not biased in favor of the rich (predominantly males) & recognizes the nature & implications of social gender inequality, greater emphasis on training & funding of legal representation for abused women, & free legal advice for victims of violence. 21 References. Coordinating Work, Family and Studies: Social Workers in Action

Author: Home, Alice1; Hinds, Cora; Malenfant, Brigitte1 School Social Work U Ottawa, Ontario K1N 6N5

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61490833?accountid=14667

Abstract: Institutional, family, & workplace variables associated with stress & strain among multiple-role women are discussed, drawing on a questionnaire survey of 169 women simultaneously holding paid employment, carrying family responsibilities, & attending a Masters social work program in Canada who were compared with a similar sample of 492 women in social work, nursing, or adult education. The majority of respondents found their course load burdensome & often experienced role conflicts when negotiating their family, work, & school obligations; these conflicts were most pronounced for the social work students. Friends & family provided the most significant support to these women, & few reported receiving much support from either work or school; some limited institutional support mechanisms are described. It is concluded that the stress of multiple-role students can be lessened through cooperation among students, institutional recognition of their special needs & problems, & information campaigns aimed at increasing student awareness of available resources. 1 Table. The Abolition of a Welfare Snitch Line

Author: Reitsma-Street, Marge1; Keck, Jennifer1 School Social Work Laurentian U Sudbury, Ontario P3E 2C6

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61424907?accountid=14667

Abstract: Examines the strategies enlisted in the elimination of a welfare snitch line (used to report welfare fraud) in Sudbury, Ontario. Drawing on public documents, observations of meetings, press clippings, & personal experiences, it is argued that this change was achieved through a three-step process. Opponents of the snitch line were mobilized through a number of informal & formal community groups & associations that stressed the unjust & unethical nature of the line, & highlighted the ways it fueled resentment against the poor. These ethical & human cost arguments were exposed to public debate via street protests, classrooms, newspaper articles, economic reports, & media talk shows, & supplemented with economic claims illustrating the financial costs of the line. Finally, decisionmakers were engaged through the strategic actions of committed groups pressing for change. These groups carefully analyzed their opportunities & documented the complex networks of opposition & support in the community. This case study provides a valuable example to other social justice groups seeking local change. 48 References. T. Sevier

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61600707?accountid=14667

Abstract: The impact of the changing work environment on student social work placements is examined in an interview survey of 18 social work supervisors of 3rd-year Bachelor of Social Work students from Carleton, Ontario. In all cases, students performed valuable work for the agencies, work that was similar to that of paid staff. For many agencies, student work was used to pilot experimental projects & initiatives that could not be attempted by paid staff in an era of cutbacks & emphasis on cost-efficiency. Most agencies were not dependent on student labor; however, supervisors often felt an obligation to educate & train students through work placements. Furthermore, work placements allowed agencies to sensitize students to issues of race, feminism, culture, etc, while promoting the beliefs, practices, & morals of the agency. Although supervision, student orientation, training, & communication with the school placed a burden on these agencies, the vast majority reported a net gain from student work. 9 References. T. Abstract: The evolution of family therapy & the Satir model is discussed in an interview with two former students of pioneer Virginia Satir who are currently family therapists utilizing the model. Abstract: Current challenges facing the Canadian foster care system are discussed in reference to the need for professionalization. The shortcomings of institutionalized care for displaced children are increasingly recognized, placing a great burden on the foster care system. It is argued that foster care has been undermined by role conflicts among foster parents who must negotiate multiple identities as parents, volunteers, clients, therapists, & staff. Furthermore, more children are entering the foster care system with special needs, & foster parents are in the best position to provide such services. Author: Smith, Deborah11 Paketiro Centre Child & Family Clinic, Wellington New Zealand

http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61567859?accountid=14667

Abstract: An analysis of sexual harassment in the workplace, focusing on the history of harassment policy in Canada. It is argued here that three models of human behavior are typically used to explain sexual harassment: the natural, in which sexual attraction may be present; the organizational, in which workplace power conflicts are expressed in demeaning sexual overtures; & the sociocultural model, in which harassment is a reflection of the inferior social status of women. Canadian policies regarding sexual harassment began in 1890, at which time factory owners were forbade from "seducing women & girls" they employed. This restriction was amended in 1920 to apply only to "girls," & was implicitly designed to protect the "virture" of the umarried female. In 1981, the code was revised to apply to females in all industries. Canadian human rights activists are currently working toward a gender-neutral policy that protects all workers from harassment. It is suggested that social workers play an important role in advocating policy changes & clarifying inconsistencies in understanding & workplace practices. 4 Tables, 21 References. http://proxy.lib.umich.edu/login?url=http://search.proquest.com/docview/61604690?accountid=14667

Abstract: In past several years, Western health care has undergone major changes in the provision of care, including the blurring of professional roles, a greater emphasis on ambulatory rather than inpatient care, & various measures designed to streamline the cost effectiveness of health service. It is argued that these constant changes present obstacles to care, which are here examined in the context of Canadian social workers. Abstract: Questionnaire data are used to examine the experiences & emotional problems of a sample of children (N = 12, ages 9+) abducted in a divorce process, & of their custodial parent, drawn from records of agencies in Canada & the US that assist parents in retrieving children.
